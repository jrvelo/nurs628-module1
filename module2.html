‚úì Diagnosis Established

In Module 1, we used the validity framework to arrive at a diagnosis with confidence:

Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent Episode, Moderate (F33.1)

We established construct validity through:

  • Discriminant Validity: High-DV symptoms (worthlessness, guilt) differentiated MDD from other conditions
  • Convergent Validity: Multiple data sources confirmed each criterion; symptoms clustered into a recognizable syndrome

Now What?

We know what J.M. has. How do we decide what to do about it?

The diagnosis tells us where we are‚Äîit's our shared starting point. But MDD can be treated many different ways:

  • Medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, atypicals...)
  • Individual therapy (CBT, DBT, IPT, psychodynamic, ACT, IFS...)
  • Group therapy
  • Family therapy
  • Lifestyle interventions (exercise, sleep hygiene, nutrition...)
  • Neuromodulation (TMS, ECT...)
  • Combined approaches

How do we choose? This is where the 5 Ps framework becomes essential.

üîÑ The 5 Ps Framework

Before we apply this to treatment, let's make sure we're clear on what each "P" represents:

Notice the temporal dimension: The 5 Ps move from DISTAL (distant past) to PROXIMAL (recent/current). This matters for treatment‚Äîdifferent therapies target different time frames.

P Presenting Problem PROXIMAL / NOW
"What brought them here?"
The current symptoms, their severity, and functional impact. What the patient is experiencing right now.
P Predisposing Factors DISTAL / PAST
"What made them vulnerable?"
Historical factors that increased risk:
  • Biological: Genetics, family psychiatric history, medical conditions
  • Developmental: Developmental delays, learning differences, early trauma
  • Psychological: Attachment patterns, personality traits, cognitive styles
  • Social/ACEs: Adverse childhood experiences, unstable home, neglect, abuse
  • Prior episodes: History of this or related conditions
P Precipitating Factors PROXIMAL / RECENT
"What triggered this? Why now?"
Recent events or stressors that triggered this episode. What changed in the weeks/months before onset‚Äîthe "last straw" that activated vulnerabilities.
P Perpetuating Factors PROXIMAL / ONGOING
"What's keeping it going?"
Current factors maintaining the problem: cognitive patterns, avoidance behaviors, interpersonal dynamics, environmental stressors, physiological cycles. These are often your primary therapy targets.
P Protective Factors DISTAL + PROXIMAL
"What strengths can we build on?"
Resources for recovery‚Äîboth historical foundations and current assets:
  • Developmental/Historical: Stable childhood home, secure attachment, no significant ACEs
  • Educational: Highest level of education, intellectual resources, problem-solving capacity
  • Social: Current support network, quality of relationships, community ties
  • Psychological: Insight, motivation, coping skills, resilience, prior treatment response
  • Functional: Stable employment, housing, financial resources
üí°
Key insight: You already organized J.M.'s data for diagnosis. Now you'll organize the same data for treatment planning. Same information, different lens, different purpose.

üîÑ The Logic Behind the Categories: Bayes Returns

Why does "stable childhood" go in Protective while "chaotic childhood" goes in Predisposing? Why does "16 years sober" protect while "urges to drink" perpetuates?

It's not arbitrary. It's Bayesian logic‚Äîthe same reasoning we used for diagnosis.

üìä Remember from Module 1:

Every piece of clinical data either increases or decreases the probability of something. That's what made symptoms useful for diagnosis‚Äîthey changed our probability estimates.

The same logic applies here‚Äîbut to a different question.

The 5 Ps Are Organized by Probability Direction

PREDISPOSING
‚Üë Increases Probability

Data that increases the probability of developing the condition.

"Chaotic childhood" ‚Üí higher probability of attachment difficulties, emotion dysregulation, vulnerability to depression

PROTECTIVE
‚Üì Decreases Probability

Data that decreases the probability of poor outcomes.

"Stable childhood" ‚Üí lower probability of attachment difficulties, better foundation, more resilience

PRECIPITATING
‚Üë Activated the Vulnerability

The event that converted vulnerability into active illness.

"Current divorce" ‚Üí given her childhood history, this specific stressor activated the latent vulnerability

PERPETUATING
‚Üî Maintains Elevated Probability

Factors that keep the probability of symptoms elevated.

"Ongoing conflict with ex" ‚Üí continues to feed the depression, prevents recovery

üí°
This is why the same data can go in different Ps. "Divorce" as a childhood event increased K.R.'s baseline vulnerability (predisposing). "Divorce" happening now activated that vulnerability (precipitating). "Conflict with ex" keeps the symptoms going (perpetuating). It's all about when it happened and what probability it changes.

üéØ Connecting the Dots

Module 1: We used Bayesian reasoning to ask "What does she have?" ‚Äî symptoms changed our probability estimates about diagnosis.

Module 2: We use the same logic to ask "Why does she have it?" ‚Äî history, context, and circumstances change our probability estimates about vulnerability, triggers, and prognosis.

The framework is the same. The question is different.

üîë Same Data, Different Lens

You collected all this data in your assessment. But how you organize it‚Äîand what you pay attention to‚Äîdepends on the question you're asking.

For DIAGNOSIS, we organized by:
  • Quantitative vs. Qualitative ‚Äî PHQ-9 score of 19 vs. "feels empty"
  • Subjective vs. Objective ‚Äî Patient report vs. MSE observations
  • Positive AND Negative findings ‚Äî What's present AND what's absent
What mattered: Symptoms present, symptoms absent (no mania, no psychosis), convergence across sources, discriminant validity

Negative findings were just as important ‚Äî "Denies manic episodes" helped rule OUT bipolar
For TREATMENT PLANNING, we organize by:
  • The 5 Ps ‚Äî Presenting, Predisposing, Precipitating, Perpetuating, Protective
  • Temporal: Distal vs. Proximal ‚Äî Distant past vs. recent/current
  • Function ‚Äî What role does this data play?
What matters: Context, vulnerabilities, what triggered this, what's maintaining it, what resources exist

History becomes central ‚Äî Childhood stability (or instability) shapes the whole picture

Example ‚Äî "No history of childhood abuse or neglect":

For diagnosis? Barely relevant. Not in MDD criteria. Noted and moved on.

For treatment planning? Highly relevant. Tells us about developmental foundation‚Äîit's a protective factor.

Example ‚Äî "Denies manic/hypomanic episodes":

For diagnosis? Crucial. A negative finding that ruled OUT bipolar.

For treatment planning? Less central. Diagnosis is established. Doesn't tell us about vulnerabilities or context.

The data is all there in your assessment.
The clinical question determines which lens you use‚Äîand which data moves to the foreground.
