Part 2: Convergent & Discriminant Synthesis
Convergent Synthesis — Presenting
D.R.’s presenting picture shows strong convergence across all three data sources for a moderate-to-severe major depressive episode with prominent secondary anxiety features. Subjective data (CC, HPI, psychiatric ROS) converges with objective MSE findings: he reports depressed mood, anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, concentration difficulties, and worthlessness — and the MSE independently confirms anxious and constricted affect, psychomotor restlessness, and speech changes on emotionally laden topics. The somatic complaints (tension headaches, GI distress, shoulder tightness, tachycardia before presentations) converge with the psychological symptoms to indicate a combined mind-body stress response. Seven of nine MDD Criterion A symptoms are met, well above the five-symptom threshold, with 6 months duration exceeding the 2-week minimum. The anxiety features (performance-focused worry, restlessness, muscle tension) onset-track with the depressive episode rather than appearing independently, suggesting they are secondary to the depressive process rather than a comorbid primary anxiety disorder.
Convergent Synthesis — Predisposing
Multiple data sources converge on a longstanding vulnerability pattern rooted in conditional self-worth and performance-contingent identity. Developmental history reveals a childhood shaped by high parental expectations (gifted programs, valedictorian, “that was kind of the expectation”), with ongoing comparison to an older brother who is a surgeon. This is not just historical context — the MSE independently confirms emotional reactivity to these themes: D.R.’s speech softens and he becomes visibly more tense when discussing parents and work performance. Family psychiatric history (mother “always been a worrier,” maternal grandmother hospitalized “for her nerves”) suggests both genetic predisposition to internalizing disorders and a family modeling pattern where distress is endured rather than addressed. His developmental note that he was “always kind of stressed about school” and his current belief that he “should be able to figure this out on my own” converge to suggest long-standing schemas around self-reliance, perfectionism, and inadequacy that pre-date this episode and created the cognitive vulnerability for it.
Convergent Synthesis — Precipitating
The precipitating picture is notable for the absence of a single clear stressor. D.R. does not identify a discrete triggering event. Instead, the timeline reveals a gradual onset over 6 months with significant acceleration over the past 8–10 weeks. The promotion offer — and his decision to decline it (“I’d probably just end up proving that I can’t handle it”) — functions as both a precipitant for worsening and evidence of how his core schemas were activated. Being offered increased responsibility directly challenged his conditional self-worth framework: the promotion wasn’t experienced as an opportunity but as an exposure threat. This is clinically important because it tells us the precipitating context was internal (schema activation in response to performance demand) rather than external (loss, trauma, disruption), which has direct implications for treatment modality selection.
Convergent Synthesis — Perpetuating
The perpetuating factors show the strongest convergence across all data sources and represent the most clinically actionable part of the formulation. Multiple self-reinforcing cycles are visible: (1) Cognitive cycle: Core beliefs about inadequacy (“what’s wrong with me,” “everyone else manages fine”) drive perfectionist compensatory behaviors (rewriting emails 4–5 times, staying late) which increase fatigue and reduce performance, which confirm the inadequacy beliefs. (2) Behavioral avoidance cycle: Declining the promotion, withdrawing socially, and constricting his routine temporarily reduce anxiety but prevent corrective experiences and shrink his world, deepening depression and reinforcing beliefs that he “can’t handle it.” (3) Physiological cycle: Rumination drives initial insomnia, poor sleep drives fatigue and impaired concentration, which drives more checking behaviors, which drives more rumination at bedtime. Increased caffeine compensates for fatigue but worsens anxiety and insomnia. Increased alcohol provides short-term relief but disrupts sleep quality (contributing to terminal insomnia at 3–4 AM). (4) Interpersonal cycle: Irritability with girlfriend and social withdrawal reduce available emotional support. Fair insight with minimization (“I should be able to figure this out on my own”) delays help-seeking and reflects the same self-reliance schema that is part of the predisposing vulnerability. (5) Ongoing environmental reinforcement: Weekly calls from parents about career progress maintain the external performance pressure that activates his core schemas. The comparison to his brother (surgeon) continuously reinforces conditional self-worth. The MSE converges with these subjective patterns: circumstantial thought process about work details reflects the rumination, psychomotor restlessness reflects the anxiety component, and his fair but minimizing insight suggests limited awareness of these cycles — which is itself a perpetuating factor.
Convergent Synthesis — Protective
Protective factors converge from both subjective and objective data sources. D.R. is college-educated, has maintained stable employment for 4 years, and has been in a 2-year relationship — indicating sustained functional capacity despite current impairment. On MSE, he presents as well-groomed, cooperative, and with intact cognition, suggesting preserved executive functioning and self-care. Critically, his affect brightens when discussing his girlfriend and running, indicating preserved capacity for pleasure (mood reactivity) despite current anhedonia — a positive prognostic sign. He denies SI with no plan or intent, and there is no access to firearms. The absence of prior psychiatric history means this is a first episode with no treatment failures, which is associated with better treatment response. His decision to seek treatment, while somewhat delayed, demonstrates emerging recognition of need despite his self-reliance schema. These protective factors suggest a patient with significant strengths and resources who is likely to respond well to structured psychotherapy, particularly if the therapeutic approach directly addresses the cognitive patterns maintaining his symptoms.
Discriminant Analysis — What Points Toward Treatment?
Looking across all five P’s, the pattern discriminates clearly toward a treatment approach that targets cognitive and behavioral perpetuating factors rather than biological or interpersonal ones as the primary modality. The predisposing factors point to deeply held schemas about conditional self-worth and performance-based identity. The precipitating context was schema activation (not trauma, loss, or biological shift). The perpetuating factors are overwhelmingly cognitive-behavioral: negative automatic thoughts, compensatory perfectionism, behavioral avoidance, and self-reinforcing rumination cycles. These factors converge to indicate CBT as the primary treatment modality. What discriminates AGAINST other modalities at this stage: The absence of trauma history argues against trauma-focused approaches (CPT, PE, EMDR). The absence of interpersonal conflict as the primary precipitant argues against IPT as the first-line choice (though interpersonal elements exist in the parent and girlfriend dynamics). The absence of emotion dysregulation, impulsivity, or self-harm argues against DBT. The intact cognitive functioning and capacity for insight argue FOR a modality that relies on cognitive awareness and behavioral experimentation. What discriminates TOWARD specific CBT targets: The cognitive content is consistently self-referential and performance-focused (“I can’t handle it,” “what’s wrong with me,” “everyone else manages fine”), consistent with Beck’s depressive triad (negative view of self, world, future). The behavioral patterns (avoidance, withdrawal, perfectionist checking) are clearly maintaining cycles that behavioral activation and graded exposure could interrupt. The somatic symptoms suggest a stress response that could be addressed through relaxation training or behavioral experiments around the physiological cycle.
Part 3: Treatment Targets
Treatment Targets
1. Negative automatic thoughts about self-worth and competence (“I can’t handle it,” “everyone else manages fine,” “what’s wrong with me”) — target with cognitive restructuring, Socratic questioning, and behavioral experiments that test the accuracy of these beliefs. 2. Behavioral avoidance pattern (declined promotion, social withdrawal, constricted routine) — target with behavioral activation, graded exposure to avoided situations, and activity scheduling to restore engagement with pleasurable activities (running, cooking, socializing). 3. Perfectionist compensatory behaviors (excessive email checking, staying late) — target with behavioral experiments around “good enough” performance, examining evidence for catastrophic predictions, and response prevention for checking behaviors. 4. Rumination-insomnia cycle — target with stimulus control and sleep hygiene psychoeducation, cognitive defusion techniques for bedtime rumination, and monitoring the caffeine-anxiety-insomnia feedback loop. 5. Self-reliance schema (“I should be able to figure this out on my own”) — target as a therapy-interfering belief that may affect treatment engagement, alliance, and homework compliance. Address through psychoeducation and normalizing help-seeking. 6. Developing awareness of the predisposing vulnerability — help D.R. recognize how childhood performance expectations created the cognitive framework that makes him vulnerable, so that current symptoms are understood as schema activation rather than evidence of inadequacy.
Treatment Rationale
The 5 P’s formulation builds a construct validity argument for CBT as the primary treatment modality for D.R. The logic flows directly from formulation to intervention: Predisposing factors (conditional self-worth, performance-contingent identity, family modeling of distress tolerance) created cognitive schemas about inadequacy and self-reliance. These schemas were activated by a precipitating context (promotion offer as performance threat) that was internal/cognitive rather than external/situational. The perpetuating factors are primarily cognitive (negative automatic thoughts, rumination) and behavioral (avoidance, perfectionist checking, social withdrawal) — exactly the domains CBT is designed to address. This represents convergent evidence for CBT: the predisposing vulnerability is cognitive (schemas), the maintaining cycles are cognitive-behavioral (NATs → avoidance → confirmation), and the precipitant was schema activation. Multiple independent data sources (self-report, behavioral observation, MSE, developmental history) all point to the same mechanism. The discriminant evidence is equally important: we can articulate why other modalities are less indicated as primary treatment. This is not a case where all therapies would be “equally appropriate” — the specific pattern of predisposing and perpetuating factors discriminates toward CBT over IPT, DBT, psychodynamic, or trauma-focused approaches as the first-line intervention. The protective factors support this direction: D.R.’s preserved cognitive functioning, intact thought process, emerging insight, and capacity for emotional engagement (brightening about girlfriend, running) suggest he has the cognitive and motivational resources to engage productively in a structured, collaborative therapy that requires between-session practice and self-monitoring.
