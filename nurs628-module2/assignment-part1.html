Step 4: Convergent Validity — How Much Evidence Comes Together?

Now we read down each column independently. For each potential diagnosis, we ask: how much of the evidence that should be present is actually present? We’re assessing each diagnosis on its own terms, using the same methods (our data sources) to evaluate each one.

This is convergent validity applied to diagnosis: measures of constructs that theoretically should be related to each other are, in fact, observed to be related to each other. The diagnosis with the strongest convergence — the most findings that should be related actually being related — has the best evidentiary support.

Step 5: Discriminant Validity — What Separates the Diagnoses?

Now we read across the rows. Discriminant validity asks: at each data source level, can we distinguish between diagnoses? Evidence that supports MDD but not GAD or Bipolar II is discriminant evidence — it tells us what this is by showing us what it isn’t.

This is the other half of construct validity: measures of constructs that theoretically should not be related to each other are, in fact, observed to not be related. Notice that some data points do double duty: they converge on MDD and discriminate against the differentials simultaneously.

Step 6: Validity Synthesis

We now bring both analyses together. Convergent validity (Step 4) showed which diagnosis the evidence best supports — MDD had the most findings that should be present actually present. Discriminant validity (Step 5) showed what separates MDD from the alternatives — findings that should not be shared between diagnoses were, in fact, not shared.

Both are required. Convergence without discrimination means you can’t tell diagnoses apart. Discrimination without convergence means you’ve ruled things out but haven’t built a positive case. Together, they give you construct validity — a defensible diagnostic conclusion.

Convergent Evidence for MDD
Subjective Data:

Patient self-report (HPI), psychiatric ROS, and collateral history all converge on depressive syndrome: depressed mood, anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, concentration impairment, worthlessness, psychomotor changes, social withdrawal, and occupational decline. The content of distress is consistently self-directed across all data sources.

Objective Data:

MSE findings corroborate subjective report: constricted affect congruent with depressed mood, speech changes on emotionally laden topics, mild psychomotor restlessness, and avoidance behavior pattern (declined promotion, social withdrawal). Physical exam negative for medical contributors. Somatic ROS findings (GI symptoms, headaches, muscle tension, tachycardia) are temporally linked to psychiatric onset.

Pattern:

Four independent data sources (patient report, ROS endorsement, behavioral history, clinician observation) converge on the same diagnostic construct. This is the hallmark of strong convergent validity — different methods of measurement yielding consistent results.

Discriminant Evidence Against Differentials
Against GAD:

Three data points discriminate: (1) anxiety symptoms lack independent ≥ 6-month duration — they track with the depressive episode; (2) worry content is narrowly self-focused rather than generalized across multiple life domains; (3) patient denies longstanding worry pattern prior to current episode. GAD remains a reasonable differential because of significant symptom overlap, but the timeline and scope do not independently satisfy criteria.

Against Bipolar II:

Discriminant evidence is strong and clean: patient denies any lifetime history of elevated, expansive, or uncharacteristically irritable mood; no decreased need for sleep with increased energy; no impulsivity, grandiosity, or pressured speech — by history or on MSE. There is no family history of bipolar disorder. The Psych ROS pertinent negatives for bipolar are entirely negative. Without a documented hypomanic episode, Bipolar II cannot be diagnosed regardless of the current depressive presentation.

Key Distinction:

These two differentials illustrate different patterns of discrimination. GAD is ruled out on degree — symptoms overlap significantly but fail to meet independent diagnostic thresholds. Bipolar II is ruled out on kind — an entire required feature (hypomania) is absent from the clinical picture.

Diagnostic Conclusion

Primary Diagnosis: Major Depressive Disorder, Single Episode, Moderate
ICD-10: F32.1  •  CPT: 90792 (Psychiatric Diagnostic Evaluation)

Convergent evidence: Four independent data sources (patient self-report, psychiatric ROS endorsement pattern, psychosocial history, and mental status examination) converge on a depressive syndrome meeting 7 of 9 DSM-5 Criterion A symptoms, with significant functional impairment across occupational, social, and relational domains.

Discriminant evidence: GAD is differentiated by timeline (anxiety symptoms lack independent ≥ 6-month course), scope (worry narrowly self-focused rather than generalized), and primacy (no pre-episode worry pattern). Bipolar II is ruled out by the complete absence of any lifetime hypomanic features by history, ROS, or MSE observation.

Differential diagnoses to monitor: (1) GAD — if anxiety symptoms persist after depressive episode resolves, reassess for independent GAD diagnosis. (2) Bipolar II — maintain screening at follow-up visits, particularly if patient develops elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, or impulsive behavior, especially in response to antidepressant treatment.
