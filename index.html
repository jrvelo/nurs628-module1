But what does that mean?

How do we get from this data to a diagnosis?

The Fundamental Challenge of Psychiatry

In psychiatry, the "what" we treatthe diagnosiscannot be measured directly.

We can only describe it.

This is a fundamental difference from much of physical medicine, where the underlying pathology can often be directly observed or tested:

Physical Medicine

Strep Throat Throat culture grows S. pyogenes
Myocardial Infarction Troponin elevated, EKG changes
Diabetes HbA1c > 6.5%, fasting glucose > 126
Fracture X-ray shows break in bone
The pathology can be directly observed or measured.

Psychiatry

Major Depressive Disorder No blood test. No brain scan. No biomarker.
Generalized Anxiety No direct measurement of "anxiety."
Schizophrenia No test for hallucinations or delusions.
PTSD No objective marker for trauma response.
We describe patterns of symptoms, behaviors, and experiences.
A Historical Parallel

Consider medicine before germ theory was proven and microscopy advanced. Physicians treating infectious disease could:

  • Observe fever, pustules, inflammation, patterns of spread
  • Collect subjective data patient reports of symptoms, onset, severity
  • Recognize patterns certain symptom clusters traveled together
  • Make predictions this pattern usually leads to this outcome

But they could not directly test for the underlying cause. The bacteria were therereal, physical entitiesbut the technology to see them, culture them, and identify them didn't exist yet.

Psychiatry is in an analogous position. The neural, psychological, and social mechanisms underlying mental disorders are realbut we cannot yet directly measure them. We work with descriptions, patterns, and inferences.

What This Means for Your Practice

1
Your clinical skills matter more, not less.

Without definitive tests, your ability to gather, organize, and interpret clinical data is the primary diagnostic instrument.

2
Systematic approaches are essential.

Because we can't "just run a test," we need rigorous frameworks for collecting and weighing evidence.

3
Validity becomes critical.

We must be deliberate about what our data tells usand what it doesn't. Convergent evidence from multiple sources builds the confidence we can't get from a single definitive test.

This is why understanding clinical data typeswhat kind of evidence we're gathering, where it comes from, and what it can tell usis foundational to psychiatric practice.

The answer is construct validity. The PHQ-9 score is considered objective because the instrument has been validated:

Construct Validity
Researchers demonstrated it actually measures what it claims to measure (depression severity).
Convergent Evidence
PHQ-9 scores correlate with other depression measures (BDI, HAM-D, clinician diagnosis).
Discriminant Evidence
PHQ-9 scores don't correlate with unrelated constructs (extraversion, IQ).
Standardization
Anyone scoring the same responses gets the same number. The measurement is independent of who administers it.
The Key Insight

Validation transforms subjective content into objective measurement.

The content being measured is subjective (the patient's inner experience). But the measurement process is objective because the questions are standardized, the scoring is standardized, and the interpretation is anchored to validated cutoffs.

This is why rating scales live in the Objective Quantitative boxnot because the experiences are objective, but because the measurement of those experiences has been validated and standardized.

Because psychiatry has less quantitative data to anchor decisions, your skill in gathering and integrating qualitative databoth subjective (interviewing) and objective (MSE)becomes critical. Convergent validity across these sources is how we build diagnostic confidence without definitive lab values.

WaitWhat About Standardized Instruments?

You might be thinking: "But we use the PHQ-9, GAD-7, BDI, HAM-D... aren't those quantitative?"

Yes. We treat them as quantitative datawe get a number, we can track it over time, we can compare it to cut-off scores.

But think about what those instruments actually measure. They're still based on patient report. They're still inferences about an underlying construct we can't directly observe.

So why do we trust them as "quantitative"?

Because researchers have established their construct validity.

They've demonstrated that the items converge (they cluster together to measure the same thing) and discriminate (they distinguish depression from anxiety, from normal distress, from other conditions).

Years of psychometric research went into proving these instruments actually measure what they claim to measure.

How do we do that for our own clinical data?

When we collect a history, do an MSE, gather collateralhow do we know our findings validly represent the construct we're trying to diagnose?

The Challenge in Psychiatry

We tend to prefer quantitative data because it feels more reliable. In physical medicine, we run labs, use imagingconcrete numbers. Patient report is almost secondary to these objective measures.

But in psychiatry? We rely heavily on qualitative data.

What the patient tells us. What we observe. We are describing something, not measuring it directly. There's no blood test for depression. No scan for anxiety.

How do we systematically and scientifically evaluate data we can't measure directly?

When We Can't Measure Directly, We Use Inference

This is where construct validity comes in.

Construct Validity

How well a set of indicators represents or reflects a concept that is not directly measurable.

Our diagnoses in psychiatry are constructs.

"Major Depressive Disorder" isn't something we can point to and measure. It's a constructa concept we infer from observable indicators.

So when we evaluate our clinical findings, we're really asking two questions:

What IS it?
How do these findings fit together? Do they form a coherent pattern?
What ISN'T it?
How is this different from other conditions? What distinguishes it?

You already have language for this second question. You learned about high-value symptoms versus transdiagnostic symptoms:

High-value symptoms help you differentiate from other disorders.
Transdiagnostic symptoms show up across disordersstill important, but they don't help us define the problem.

These concepts"what is it" and "what isn't it"have formal names in research methodology. Let's look at where they come from.

Campbell & Fiske (1959): The Validity Framework

In 1959, psychologists Donald Campbell and Donald Fiske published a landmark paper that changed how we think about measurement validity.

Campbell, D.T., & Fiske, D.W. (1959). Convergent and discriminant validation by the multitrait-multimethod matrix. Psychological Bulletin, 56(2), 81-105.

The Problem They Were Solving

Researchers had been struggling with a fundamental question: How do you know if you're actually measuring what you think you're measuring?

If you create a test for "depression," how do you know it's really measuring depression and not just general distress, or anxiety, or something else entirely?

Their Key Insight

Campbell and Fiske realized that establishing construct validity requires demonstrating two things:

1
Convergent Validity
Formal Definition:

"The degree to which concepts that should be related theoretically are, in fact, related."

Plain Language:

Things that should go together actually DO go together.

Example:

If someone has depression, we'd expect depressed mood, anhedonia, sleep problems, and hopelessness to appear togetherthey converge.

2
Discriminant Validity
Formal Definition:

"The degree to which concepts that should not be related theoretically are, in fact, not related."

Plain Language:

Things that should be different actually ARE different.

Example:

Depression and anxiety are different constructs. A valid measure of depression should discriminate it from anxiety.

Why You Need Both

Here's the critical point from Campbell & Fiske:

"For the justification of novel trait measures, for the validation of test interpretation, or for the establishment of construct validity, discriminant validation as well as convergent validation is required."

Campbell & Fiske, 1959, p. 81

Why do you need both? Because having just one isn't enough:

Discriminant Validity: Does This Finding Help Me Differentiate?

Remember Campbell & Fiske's definition:

Discriminant Validity

"The degree to which concepts that should not be related theoretically are, in fact, not related."

In clinical terms, this translates to a simple question:

Does this finding help me tell the difference between conditions?

The Language You Already Know

You've already learned this conceptjust with different words:

A symptom with high discriminant validity is one that helps you differentiate one disorder from others. It's relatively specific to that condition.

A symptom with low discriminant validity appears across many conditions. It tells you something is wrong, but not what.

But How Do We Quantify This?

Saying a symptom is "high value" or "transdiagnostic" is useful, but it's still somewhat subjective. How do researchers actually measure discriminant validity? How do we move from intuition to evidence?

The answer comes from psychometric properties you may have encountered in your research courses. Let's work through this step by step.

Step 1: Two Basic Questions About Any Symptom

When evaluating how useful a symptom is for diagnosis, researchers ask two fundamental questions:

Sensitivity

When the disorder IS present, how often does this symptom appear?

High sensitivity = the symptom catches most people who have the condition

If sensitivity = 95%, then 95% of people with the disorder have this symptom.

Specificity

When the disorder is NOT present, how often is this symptom absent?

High specificity = the symptom is rare in people without the condition

If specificity = 90%, then only 10% of people without the disorder have this symptom.

Step 2: The Problem With Using Just One

Here's why you can't rely on sensitivity or specificity alone:

High Sensitivity, Low Specificity

Example: "Fatigue" for depression

Sensitivity ~85%: Most depressed patients report fatigue.

Specificity ~30%: But so do patients with anxiety, medical illness, sleep disorders, and healthy people who are just tired.

Bottom line: Finding fatigue doesn't tell you muchit could be almost anything.

High Specificity, Low Sensitivity

Example: "Psychomotor retardation" for depression

Specificity ~90%: When you see it, it strongly suggests depression.

Sensitivity ~40%: But most depressed patients don't show observable psychomotor slowing.

Bottom line: Useful when present, but absence doesn't rule out depression.

What we really need is a way to combine these two pieces of information into a single metric that answers: "When I find this symptom, how much should it change my thinking?"

But before we get to that metric, we need to understand what "change my thinking" actually means. This requires a different way of reasoning about diagnosis.

Introducing Bayesian Reasoning
What is Bayesian Reasoning?

Named after Thomas Bayes, an 18th-century statistician, Bayesian reasoning is simply this:

You start with an initial belief, then update that belief as you gather new evidence.

This might sound obvious, but it's actually different from how we often think about diagnosis. Let's compare:

Non-Bayesian Thinking

"The patient has 5 of 9 MDD symptoms, so they have MDD."

Problem: Treats diagnosis as a checklist. Doesn't account for how strong each piece of evidence is.

Bayesian Thinking

"Given the setting, my initial estimate was 30%. But finding worthlessness and guiltsymptoms that are very specific to depressionraises my confidence to 85%."

Benefit: Each piece of evidence carries different weight. Some findings shift your thinking more than others.

An Everyday Example

You actually use Bayesian reasoning all the time without realizing it. Consider this:

Will it rain today?
Prior belief:
You check your phoneit's April in Phoenix. Base rate of rain is about 10%.
New evidence:
You look outside and see dark clouds. Dark clouds are much more common on rainy days than sunny days.
Updated belief:
Now you think there's maybe a 40% chance of rain. You grab an umbrella.
More evidence:
You step outside and smell that distinctive "rain smell" (petrichor). That's very specific to impending rain.
Final belief:
Now you're 80% sure it's going to rain. You text your friend to move your coffee date indoors.
Notice what happened: You didn't just say "clouds = rain." You started with a base rate, then updated as you gathered more information. Some evidence (the smell) shifted your belief more than other evidence (the clouds) because it's more specific to rain.

Clinical diagnosis works exactly the same way.

Bayesian Reasoning in Clinical Practice
1
Start with a Prior Probability
Before you find any symptoms, what's the baseline likelihood this patient has a given condition? This comes from:
  • Base rates: How common is this condition in general?
  • Clinical setting: A psychiatric clinic vs. an ER vs. primary care
  • Initial presentation: What brought them in?
  • Demographics: Age, gender, risk factors
Example: A 52-year-old woman presenting to a psychiatric clinic with "feeling down" your prior probability for MDD might start around 30-40%.
2
Update with Each Finding
Each clinical finding you discover shifts that probability up or down. But by how much?
This is where we need a tool something that tells us the weight of each piece of evidence. How much should finding "worthlessness" shift our probability compared to finding "insomnia"?
3
Arrive at a Posterior Probability
After considering all your findings, you have an updated probability your diagnostic confidence.
After finding worthlessness, guilt, anhedonia, and ruling out mania/psychosis, your probability of MDD might be >95%. That's enough confidence to make the diagnosis.
The key question: In Step 2, we said each finding "shifts" our probability. But how do we know how much to shift? We need a tool that quantifies the weight of each piece of evidence. That tool is the Likelihood Ratio.
Important Limitations of Bayesian Reasoning

Bayesian reasoning is powerful, but it's not magic. Be aware of these limitations:

1. It assumes you've considered all possibilities
Bayesian reasoning updates probabilities across a set of hypothesesbut only the ones you're considering. If the true diagnosis isn't in your differential, no amount of Bayesian updating will get you there. This is why broad differential diagnosis remains essential.
2. Prior probabilities can be subjective
Where does your "30% prior for MDD" come from? Base rates from research, clinical setting, patient demographics, your gut feeling? Different clinicians might start with different priors. The framework doesn't eliminate subjectivityit makes your assumptions explicit.
3. LR+ values aren't always available
We calculated discriminant validity for DSM symptoms because the DSM's structure makes this tractablewe know which symptoms appear in which disorders. For many clinical findings, you won't have precise LR+ values and will need to estimate based on clinical experience.
4. Symptoms aren't independent
Technically, you can only multiply LR+ values if findings are independent. In reality, symptoms clusterworthlessness and guilt tend to co-occur. This means the math is an approximation, not a precise calculation.
The bottom line: Bayesian reasoning is a framework for thinking systematically about evidence, not a calculator that gives you "the answer." The goal is to make your clinical reasoning more explicit, rigorous, and defensiblenot to replace clinical judgment.
Why our analysis is more straightforward: For the symptom discriminant validity analysis we'll show you, we used the DSM's own structure. We didn't estimate priors subjectivelywe counted how many disorders include each symptom as a criterion. This gives us an objective measure of specificity based on the diagnostic system itself.

Key Concept

The LR+ is not the probability of having the condition. It's a multiplier that tells you how much to adjust your probability when you find that symptom. A symptom with LR+ of 50 doesn't mean 50% probabilityit means finding that symptom makes the condition 50 times more likely than it was before.

Why this matters clinically: The same finding can mean different things in different contexts. Worthlessness in a patient presenting to an outpatient psychiatry clinic (prior ~30% for MDD) means something different than worthlessness in a patient presenting to an ER after a car accident (prior maybe ~5% for MDD). The LR+ is the same, but your posterior probability is different because you started from a different place.

Now let's look at what different LR+ values mean for updating your probability:

LR+ = 1 The symptom is equally common in people with and without the disorder. It tells you nothing.
LR+ = 5 The symptom is 5 times more likely in people with the disorder. Now we're getting somewhere.
LR+ = 20 The symptom is 20 times more likely in people with the disorder. This is a powerful diagnostic clue.
LR+ = 50 The symptom is 50 times more likely in people with the disorder. Finding this should strongly shift your diagnostic thinking.

The standard interpretation scale:

Likelihood Ratio (LR+) Interpretation

How much does finding this symptom shift the probability of the diagnosis?

LR+ 1-2 Minimal change in probability
LR+ 2-5 Small increase in probability
LR+ 5-10 Moderate increase in probability
LR+ >10 Large increase in probability
Step 4: From LR+ to Discriminant Validity

Now we can make the connection explicit:

A symptom with a high LR+ is one that is much more common in people with the disorder than in people without it.

That means when you find it, it discriminatesit helps you differentiate this disorder from other conditions.

In other words:

High LR+ Symptom differentiates this disorder from others High Discriminant Validity

And conversely:

Low LR+ (near 1) Symptom appears across many conditions Low Discriminant Validity (Transdiagnostic)
This is the quantitative foundation for "high-value" vs. "transdiagnostic" symptoms. When we say a symptom has "high discriminant validity," we're saying it has empirical dataa high LR+showing it differentiates one condition from others. It's not just clinical intuition; it's measurable.

We Did the Analysis

This isn't just theory. We analyzed over 1,400 symptom-disorder pairs across DSM-5-TR disorders using these psychometric methods to determine their discriminant validity.

Here's how we did itand you can follow the logic yourself:

Our Methodology: Using the DSM Against Itself
1
Extract All Diagnostic Criteria
We pulled every symptom from every disorder's diagnostic criteria in the DSM-5-TR. This gave us a complete map of which symptoms belong to which disorders.
2
Count Symptom Overlap
For each symptom, we counted: How many different disorders include this symptom as a criterion?

Example: "Insomnia" appears as a criterion in MDD, GAD, PTSD, Bipolar, Adjustment Disorder, and several others.

Example: "Feelings of worthlessness" appears primarily in MDDit's rare in other disorders.

3
Calculate Specificity from Overlap
The key insight: Specificity is inversely related to how many disorders share a symptom.

If a symptom appears in many disorders LOW specificity it doesn't help you differentiate

If a symptom appears in few disorders HIGH specificity it DOES help you differentiate

4
Calculate Likelihood Ratios
Using the sensitivity and specificity values, we calculated LR+ for each symptom-disorder pair:

LR+ = Sensitivity (1 - Specificity)

5
Apply Base Rate Assumptions
We tested how well symptoms discriminate under different clinical scenarios:
10% Base Rate
General population or primary care setting
How well does this symptom discriminate when the disorder is relatively rare?
30% Base Rate
Psychiatric clinic setting
How well does this symptom discriminate when the disorder is more common?
6
Categorize Discriminant Validity
Based on the LR+ values, we categorized each symptom:
HIGHLY DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ > 100 Very strong diagnostic signal (specificity > 99%)
MODERATELY DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ 20-100 Strong, useful signal
LESS DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ < 20 Transdiagnostic, weaker signal
Important: "Moderately Discriminative" doesn't mean "not useful"these symptoms (LR+ 20-100) still carry significant diagnostic weight. An LR+ of 50 means finding that symptom makes the diagnosis 50 times more likely. The "Less Discriminative" symptoms are the transdiagnostic ones that appear across many conditions.
The elegant part: We didn't need external data to do this. The DSM itself tells us which symptoms are specific and which are transdiagnosticwe just had to analyze its own structure systematically. The DSM's criteria reveal their own discriminant validity.

Convergent Validity: Do These Findings Fit Together?

Remember Campbell & Fiske's definition:

Convergent Validity

"The degree to which concepts that should be related theoretically are, in fact, related."

In clinical terms, this translates to:

Do my findings cluster together into a coherent, recognizable pattern?

The Other Half of the Equation

Discriminant validity asks: "Does this finding help me differentiate?"

Convergent validity asks: "Do my findings fit together?"

Discriminant Validity

What ISN'T it?

Focuses on individual findings and their diagnostic weight

Asks: Does this symptom help me rule in or rule out?

Convergent Validity

What IS it?

Focuses on the pattern of findings and how they cluster

Asks: Do these pieces fit together into a syndrome?

What Does "Convergence" Look Like Clinically?

When findings converge, they point toward the same construct from multiple angles:

Signs of Convergent Validity in Clinical Data
Consistency Across Sources
Patient report matches your observations matches collateral information
Consistency Across Domains
Cognitive symptoms + emotional symptoms + behavioral symptoms + physical symptoms all point the same direction
Temporal Coherence
Symptoms emerged together, wax and wane together, fit a recognizable timeline
Fits the Clinical Picture
The pattern matches what we know about how this disorder presents

What Undermines Convergence?

Low convergent validityfindings that don't fit togetheris a clinical red flag:

Inconsistency across sources: Patient says one thing, you observe another, collateral reports something different
Symptoms from different syndromes: Some findings point to depression, others to mania, others to psychosisthey don't cluster
Temporal incoherence: The timeline doesn't make sense for this disorder
Atypical presentation: The pattern doesn't match what we'd expectconsider alternative diagnoses or comorbidity
Low convergence doesn't mean the patient isn't suffering. It means the clinical picture is complexmaybe there's comorbidity, maybe there's an atypical presentation, maybe you need more information. It's a signal to keep investigating, not to dismiss.

So What Have We Actually Done?

We've arrived at a diagnosis: Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent Episode, Moderate.

Let's be clear about what this is and what it isn't.

The Diagnosis: A Shared Starting Point

The diagnosis is agnostic. It tells us where we arenot where to go. It defines the problem, not the solution.

The DSM isn't perfect. There are many valid criticismscategorical vs. dimensional, cultural bias, comorbidity problems, the medicalization of human suffering. But it is the language we have all agreed on. It's how we communicate with each other, with insurance companies, with the healthcare system.

The diagnosis brings us all to the same starting point. When you say "MDD," another clinician knows what you mean. You can collaborate. You can consult. You can refer. You speak the same language.

Your Role in Getting Here

Think about what it took to arrive at this diagnosis:

Therapeutic rapport. Open-ended questions. Your ability to understand and connect with your patient. Making them feel safe enough to tell you what's really going on. Knowing what to ask, when to probe deeper, when to sit with silence.

All of thatthe art of the clinical interviewis data collection.

It's like the cooking show "Nailed It"everyone's trying to replicate the same design. How you get there, how sound and solid your foundation is, that's between you and your patient. But it is on you to do the best job possible collecting quality data.

The validity framework we've learned gives you a systematic way to evaluate that data. But the quality of your starting point depends on your skill at gathering it.
