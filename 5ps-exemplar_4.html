Part 2: Working Formulation — What Do We Think Is Going On?
Formulation Summary
Presenting Presenting concerns consistent with diagnosis of MDD including depressed mood, anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, concentration problems, and worthlessness. On exam, affect is anxious and constricted, consistent with self-report. He also notes significant worry about his performance at work and meeting expectations. Somatic complaints present (tension headaches, GI distress, muscle tension) — appear stress-related. Anxiety features seem to track with the depressive timeline — will need to monitor whether this warrants separate attention.
Predisposing Predisposing factors include family history of anxiety — mother “always a worrier,” maternal grandmother hospitalized “for her nerves.” There was a lot of pressure to do well growing up — gifted programs, valedictorian, frequent comparison to older brother who is a surgeon. Home life was stable however. On exam he gets noticeably tense and his voice drops when talking about parents and work, suggesting these themes are still active. Reports he was “always kind of stressed about school.” No prior psychiatric history.
Precipitating Main precipitating factor appears to be increasing demands at work. Symptoms have progressively worsened over 6 months with acceleration over the last 8–10 weeks. Was offered a promotion and declined — “I’d probably just end up proving I can’t handle it.” No other clear precipitating factors noted — no loss, trauma, relationship change, or medical event.
Perpetuating Suspected perpetuating factors include significant worry around not measuring up — “everyone else manages fine,” “what’s wrong with me.” He is compensating by overworking (rewriting emails 4–5 times, staying late) which does not seem to be helping. He is isolating — declining invitations, dropped running and cooking. He is not getting restful sleep — ruminating at bedtime and waking at 3–4 AM. He is drinking more coffee to compensate and slightly more alcohol on weekends — both of these pieces independently are likely perpetuating factors. His belief that he “should be able to figure this out on my own” may also be a factor.
Protective Employed and functioning at work, though with difficulty. In a stable 2-year relationship. Affect brightens when discussing girlfriend and running — capacity for pleasure still present. No SI. No prior psychiatric history — first episode, no treatment failures. Presented for treatment on his own.
What Else Do We Need to Know?
(what would help us discriminate between treatment options)		 Would want more information on: family dynamics growing up and current relationship with parents (weekly calls about career — how does he experience those?). Current supports beyond girlfriend. Stability of interpersonal relationships — is the irritability with girlfriend new or longstanding? His personal goals for treatment. Whether the anxiety features resolve as depression improves or persist independently. Baseline PHQ-9 and GAD-7 to establish severity.
Part 3: Preliminary Treatment Considerations
Treatment Targets
Possible treatment targets include: 1. Pt’s rumination around not being good enough — the “what’s wrong with me” and “everyone else manages fine” thinking. 2. Pattern of avoidance — declining the promotion, pulling away from friends, dropping activities he used to enjoy. 3. Perfectionist checking at work — the email rewriting and staying late. 4. Sleep — rumination at bedtime is driving the insomnia, and the poor sleep is making everything else harder. 5. Improved coping strategies — the caffeine and alcohol are probably complicating this.
Treatment Rationale
Main concerns appear to center around how pt thinks about himself, how he copes with stress, and how those patterns are affecting his functioning and relationships. Predisposing history suggests this has been building for a long time but was manageable until work demands increased. This formulation is our working hypothesis. In the next step, we’ll use it to evaluate which specific therapy modality is the best match for D.R.’s clinical picture.
