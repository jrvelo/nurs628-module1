3 Treatment Targets
3A: Core CBT Treatment Targets

1. Guilt, feelings of worthlessness. Patient statements of “I’m not sure I have value outside of being useful to other people” consistent with cognitive distortions that can be targeted through CBT. The “what could I have done differently” loop is a ruminative pattern that CBT can address through cognitive restructuring and examining evidence.

2. Behavioral withdrawal and avoidance. She has stopped attending book club, church, and social events. Behavioral activation component of CBT targets the avoidance maintaining her depression. Her pattern of focusing on others’ needs functions as avoidance of her own emotional experience. CBT can help her recognize this as a behavioral pattern and develop alternatives.

3B: Individual CBT — What This Format Adds

Sustained exploration of the caretaking schema. The belief that “if everyone around me was okay, then I was okay” appears to be a longstanding pattern rooted in childhood. Individual CBT may provide the sustained, private space needed to trace this from childhood household management through her marriage and career, and to begin developing an alternative self-concept. This kind of work may require a level of safety and depth that individual format supports.

Processing divorce-specific cognitions. The “replaceable” cognition and self-blame rumination involve painful, private content. Individual CBT would allow uninterrupted exploration of these automatic thoughts through thought records and Socratic questioning without concern about group dynamics or self-censoring.

Risk of caretaking pattern activation. M.T. herself identified this: “I’d probably spend the whole time worrying about everyone else.” Individual format removes the opportunity for this avoidance pattern to activate during session — though whether that is an advantage or a disadvantage depends on how you think about it (see 3C).

3C: Group CBT — What This Format Adds

Universality may directly challenge isolation and shame. M.T.’s withdrawal appears driven by the belief that she’s “the sad divorced friend” and that no one thinks of her as someone who needs help. Hearing others share similar struggles could provide behavioral evidence that disconfirms these beliefs — the kind of corrective experience that individual therapy may not be able to replicate.

Caretaking pattern may become visible and addressable in real time. This may be the critical discriminant consideration. If M.T. starts focusing on other members’ needs, the pattern is happening live in the room. A skilled group leader could name it, the group could reflect it back, and M.T. would get corrective feedback in the moment rather than talking about the pattern in the abstract. This is the flip side of the 3B concern — the same pattern that could be a liability in group is also what makes group uniquely suited to address it.

Identity reconstruction through social mirror. M.T.’s identity concern (“I don’t know who I am”) appears interpersonal at its core. Group could provide a context where she practices being seen as more than “useful” — where she receives without giving, is vulnerable without caretaking, and begins to discover what she needs rather than just what others need.

Instillation of hope. Seeing other members at different stages of working through similar losses could provide motivation and model that recovery is possible — something she may not be able to access from her current social network given her withdrawal.

Interpersonal learning and feedback. Group members could provide honest feedback about how M.T.’s deflection and incongruent smiling affect them — the kind of feedback she likely does not receive in her current relationships.

4 Clinical Formulation
Brief Summary
M.T. is a 43-year-old female presenting with an 18-month history of depressed mood precipitated by a divorce, with symptom worsening over the past 4–5 months. She previously sought therapy following the death of her mother, which she found helpful; no formal diagnosis was made at that time, and she has no other history of mental health treatment. Current presenting symptoms include anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, guilt, difficulty concentrating, and feelings of worthlessness. Sleep disturbance (sleep-onset insomnia) is present and may be exacerbating daytime fatigue and concentration difficulties. PHQ-9 score is 16, consistent with moderately severe depression.
Biological Factors
No significant medical history. Family psychiatric history is notable for maternal depression and a sister currently taking an SSRI. No current medications. No past or present substance use concerns.
Psychosocial Factors
Childhood was generally stable; however, the patient assumed a caregiver role for her siblings, as parents were frequently absent due to work obligations, resulting in an overall lack of emotional support. Currently, she is maintaining employment and parenting responsibilities but has withdrawn from her social network, discontinued previously enjoyed activities, and reports uncertainty about her identity in light of recent life changes.
Diagnosis & Core Treatment Targets
Presentation is most consistent with Major Depressive Disorder, single episode, moderate severity. Although the patient endorses a recent diminished sense of self and social withdrawal, her relationships remain broadly stable, and there is no evidence of a longstanding pattern of identity disturbance. (Note to students: Here I am ruling out personality disorder; symptoms are most parsimoniously accounted for by the depressive episode.)

5 Treatment Targets & Modality Selection
Treatment Targets & Modality Selection

Core treatment targets include feelings of guilt, worthlessness, and social withdrawal, which appear to be the primary perpetuating factors maintaining the depressive episode.

Patient statements consistent with cognitive distortions include: “I’m not sure I have value outside of being useful to other people,” “What could I have done differently?” and “I don’t want to be the sad divorced friend.” Her history of assuming a caregiver role in childhood suggests maladaptive schemas around worth and security that may be reinforcing these distortions.

Both individual and group CBT would be appropriate for this patient. Evidence consistently indicates comparable outcomes across formats, and the patient would likely benefit from elements of both (Söchting, 2023). Individual CBT would allow her to address the sensitive areas of loss and identity disruption with greater depth and privacy than a group setting. Group CBT would offer therapeutic factors less available in individual work — including instillation of hope, corrective recapitulation, interpersonal learning, group cohesiveness, and catharsis — that could help her process the divorce, reduce social isolation, and address existential concerns.

Given the sensitive nature of the precipitating event, the recommendation is to begin with individual CBT to process the divorce and stabilize core symptoms, with transition to group CBT thereafter. This sequencing is consistent with the most current NICE guidelines (2022), which recommend individual CBT for more severe and new presentations. However, each format addresses different aspects of the precipitating and perpetuating factors, and there is a lack of evidence specifically evaluating individual versus group versus combined CBT for this clinical scenario. Ultimately, both options should be presented to the patient using a shared decision-making approach.
