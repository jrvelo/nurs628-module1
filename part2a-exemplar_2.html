▼ Now use the formulation to identify treatment targets — first what CBT can address regardless of format, then what each format uniquely offers.
3 Treatment Targets
3A: Core CBT Treatment Targets

1. Automatic thoughts around worthlessness and identity. “I’m not sure I have value outside of being useful to other people.” This is a core cognition driving her depressed mood. CBT can help her identify, examine, and test this belief through thought records, behavioral experiments, and examining the evidence for and against it.

2. Behavioral withdrawal and avoidance. She has stopped book club, church, yoga invitations, and social events. She keeps Diane’s calls short. Behavioral activation directly targets the avoidance cycle — the withdrawal reduces access to positive reinforcement and social support, which deepens the depression.

3. Self-blame and ruminative thinking about the divorce. The “what could I have done differently” loop is a ruminative pattern that drives bedtime insomnia and maintains depressed mood. CBT can address this through cognitive restructuring — identifying thinking traps (personalization, “should” statements) and developing balanced appraisals.

4. Caretaking as avoidance of own emotional needs. Her pattern of focusing on others functions as avoidance of her own emotional experience. This is both behavioral (she fills her time with others’ needs) and cognitive (she believes she must be useful to have value). CBT can help her recognize this as a pattern, identify the automatic thoughts driving it, and develop alternative responses.

5. Sleep onset insomnia. Driven by nighttime rumination. Cognitive and behavioral strategies targeting sleep (stimulus control, sleep restriction, cognitive strategies for managing bedtime worry) are evidence-based and directly applicable.

3B: Individual CBT — What This Format Adds

Sustained exploration of the caretaking schema. The belief that “if everyone around me was okay, then I was okay” appears to be a longstanding pattern rooted in childhood. Individual CBT may provide the sustained, private space needed to trace this from childhood household management through her marriage and career, and to begin developing an alternative self-concept. This kind of work may require a level of safety and depth that individual format supports.

Processing divorce-specific cognitions. The “replaceable” cognition and self-blame rumination involve painful, private content. Individual CBT would allow uninterrupted exploration of these automatic thoughts through thought records and Socratic questioning without concern about group dynamics or self-censoring.

Risk of caretaking pattern activation. M.T. herself identified this: “I’d probably spend the whole time worrying about everyone else.” Individual format removes the opportunity for this avoidance pattern to activate during session — though whether that is an advantage or a disadvantage depends on how you think about it (see 3C).

3C: Group CBT — What This Format Adds

Universality may directly challenge isolation and shame. M.T.’s withdrawal appears driven by the belief that she’s “the sad divorced friend” and that no one thinks of her as someone who needs help. Hearing others share similar struggles could provide behavioral evidence that disconfirms these beliefs — the kind of corrective experience that individual therapy may not be able to replicate.

Caretaking pattern may become visible and addressable in real time. This may be the critical discriminant consideration. If M.T. starts focusing on other members’ needs, the pattern is happening live in the room. A skilled group leader could name it, the group could reflect it back, and M.T. would get corrective feedback in the moment rather than talking about the pattern in the abstract. This is the flip side of the 3B concern — the same pattern that could be a liability in group is also what makes group uniquely suited to address it.

Identity reconstruction through social mirror. M.T.’s identity concern (“I don’t know who I am”) appears interpersonal at its core. Group could provide a context where she practices being seen as more than “useful” — where she receives without giving, is vulnerable without caretaking, and begins to discover what she needs rather than just what others need.

Instillation of hope. Seeing other members at different stages of working through similar losses could provide motivation and model that recovery is possible — something she may not be able to access from her current social network given her withdrawal.

Interpersonal learning and feedback. Group members could provide honest feedback about how M.T.’s deflection and incongruent smiling affect them — the kind of feedback she likely does not receive in her current relationships.

4 Clinical Formulation
Brief Summary
M.T. is a 43-year-old female school counselor presenting with an 18-month history of depressive symptoms that have worsened over the past 4–5 months following her husband-initiated divorce. She reports persistent low mood, anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, guilt, concentration difficulties, and feelings of worthlessness tied to identity and role loss. PHQ-9 of 16 (moderately severe). She is maintaining employment and parenting responsibilities but describes herself as “running on autopilot.” Significant social withdrawal appears driven by shame about her post-divorce status.
Biological Factors
Family psychiatric history notable for maternal depression (untreated, “bad spells, especially in winter”) and sister currently on SSRI. Hypothyroidism present but well-controlled (TSH 2.1 on levothyroxine) — should be rechecked given current depressive episode, as subclinical changes could be contributing. Vitamin D low-normal (28) — supplementation may be warranted. No current psychiatric medications. Sleep disruption (5–6 hours) and mild weight gain may be both contributing to and resulting from the depressive episode. No substance concerns.
Psychosocial Factors
M.T.’s presentation appears best understood through the interaction of a longstanding caretaking pattern with an acute loss that dismantled the framework it depended on. She developed a self-concept organized around being useful to others during childhood, when she took on household responsibilities while her mother worked nights. This was largely adaptive for decades — she built a career in school counseling, a 16-year marriage, and a social network around it. The divorce removed the central structure, and the ex-husband’s quick new relationship may have activated the belief that she was “replaceable” — which, for someone whose value appears defined by usefulness, would be a particularly painful cognition. Suspected perpetuating factors include social withdrawal driven by shame, which reduces access to support and positive reinforcement. The caretaking pattern continues at work, leaving her depleted with nothing left for self-reflection. Self-blame rumination appears to maintain depressed mood and may be driving the insomnia. She absorbs her daughter’s anger without boundaries. Her identity remains organized around being useful to others with no alternative self-concept developed, and the incongruent smiling and deflection visible on exam likely prevent authentic connection in her current relationships. Available supports exist — Diane, colleagues, faith community, sister — but her shame-driven withdrawal keeps them at arm’s length.
Diagnostic Formulation
Working diagnosis is Major Depressive Disorder, single episode, moderate. M.T. appears to meet DSM-5 criteria with depressed mood, anhedonia, insomnia, fatigue, guilt, concentration difficulty, and worthlessness present for well over two weeks with significant functional impact. PHQ-9 of 16 supports moderately severe severity. Mild anxiety symptoms (GAD-7: 8) are present but appear situational and secondary to the depressive episode rather than representing a comorbid anxiety disorder — they center specifically on children and future rather than being generalized. Adjustment disorder was considered given the clear precipitant, but symptom severity, duration (18 months), and the degree to which predisposing factors appear to contribute to the presentation support MDD. Persistent depressive disorder could be considered if the symptoms have been more chronic than M.T. reports, but her description of a clear pre-morbid baseline and identifiable onset argues against it. Thyroid should be rechecked to rule out contribution. No evidence of bipolar features, psychotic symptoms, PTSD, or personality disorder, though the caretaking pattern warrants ongoing observation.

5 Treatment Targets & Modality Selection
Convergent Targets (both formats address)

Cognitive restructuring of worthlessness/self-blame, behavioral activation to counter withdrawal, psychoeducation on depression and the cognitive model, sleep-related cognitive strategies. These confirm CBT as the right modality but don’t differentiate between individual and group.

Discriminant Targets (favor one format)

Favor group: Universality to counter shame-driven isolation. Real-time visibility of the caretaking pattern. Identity reconstruction through interpersonal feedback. Instillation of hope from peers at different recovery stages.

Favor individual: Sustained schema exploration of childhood caretaking roots. Private processing of divorce-related cognitions.

Treatment Targets & Modality Selection

M.T.’s five core CBT treatment targets are: (1) automatic thoughts around worthlessness and identity, (2) behavioral withdrawal driven by shame, (3) self-blame rumination about the divorce, (4) caretaking as avoidance of her own emotional needs, and (5) sleep onset insomnia. All five appear accessible to CBT intervention. The question is which format — individual or group — may be the better initial approach.

Both individual and group CBT can address cognitive restructuring of worthlessness cognitions and behavioral activation to counter her withdrawal. These are convergent targets — they support CBT as the right modality but do not help discriminate between formats. The discriminant targets appear to favor group CBT as the initial intervention for M.T., for several reasons.

First, M.T.’s shame-driven social withdrawal appears to be a primary perpetuating factor. She has isolated herself based on the belief that she is “the sad divorced friend” and that “no one thinks of me as someone who needs help.” Group CBT could provide a corrective experience that individual therapy may not be able to replicate: hearing others share similar struggles would provide behavioral evidence that disconfirms these beliefs. This maps to Yalom’s universality factor, which research consistently identifies as one of the most valued therapeutic experiences in group therapy (Yalom & Leszcz, 2020). For M.T., universality would not just be a nice addition — it would target the shame that appears to be driving her withdrawal.

Second, M.T.’s caretaking pattern appears to be the thread connecting her predisposing factors to her current perpetuating factors. In individual therapy, this pattern would likely be discussed in the abstract. In group, it may be activated in real time — and M.T. already suspects this: “I’d probably spend the whole time worrying about everyone else instead of focusing on myself.” This self-awareness may actually be an indicator of readiness. If the pattern activates in group, it becomes observable, nameable, and addressable in the moment. A skilled group leader could invite M.T. to notice what she is doing, and group members could reflect back how her deflection affects them — the kind of feedback she likely does not receive in her current relationships. This corrective interpersonal experience is identified as a primary mechanism of change in group therapy (Bieling et al., 2006) and may represent something individual CBT cannot provide in the same way.

Third, M.T.’s identity concern appears interpersonal at its core. She does not know who she is outside of being useful to others. Group could provide a context where she practices being seen as more than “useful” — where she receives support, is vulnerable, and begins to discover what she needs. This maps to what Yalom describes as interpersonal learning and group cohesiveness — the sense of belonging and acceptance that can become a laboratory for developing new ways of relating.

The discriminant targets favoring individual CBT — sustained schema exploration and private divorce processing — are clinically important. However, they may be better suited as a secondary or concurrent intervention once M.T. has begun to re-engage socially and has experienced the corrective interpersonal feedback that group provides. The schema work in particular may be more meaningful once she has had the lived experience of being valued for something other than caretaking.

Recommendation: Group CBT as the initial intervention, with consideration for adding individual CBT concurrently or sequentially for targeted schema work. M.T. appears to have multiple indicators of group readiness: openness to the format, capacity for self-reflection about her own patterns, good interpersonal skills, motivation, and a presentation (moderate depression without SI, personality disorder, or active substance use) that would be appropriate for outpatient group. The group format appears to most directly target her primary perpetuating factors — shame-driven isolation, caretaking avoidance, and identity loss — through mechanisms that individual therapy alone may not provide.
