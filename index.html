But what does that mean?

How do we get from this data to a diagnosis?

The Fundamental Challenge of Psychiatry

In psychiatry, the "what" we treatthe diagnosiscannot be measured directly.

We can only describe it.

This is a fundamental difference from much of physical medicine, where the underlying pathology can often be directly observed or tested:

Physical Medicine

Strep Throat Throat culture grows S. pyogenes
Myocardial Infarction Troponin elevated, EKG changes
Diabetes HbA1c > 6.5%, fasting glucose > 126
Fracture X-ray shows break in bone
The pathology can be directly observed or measured.

Psychiatry

Major Depressive Disorder No blood test. No brain scan. No biomarker.
Generalized Anxiety No direct measurement of "anxiety."
Schizophrenia No test for hallucinations or delusions.
PTSD No objective marker for trauma response.
We describe patterns of symptoms, behaviors, and experiences.
A Historical Parallel

Consider medicine before germ theory was proven and microscopy advanced. Physicians treating infectious disease could:

  • Observe fever, pustules, inflammation, patterns of spread
  • Collect subjective data patient reports of symptoms, onset, severity
  • Recognize patterns certain symptom clusters traveled together
  • Make predictions this pattern usually leads to this outcome

But they could not directly test for the underlying cause. The bacteria were therereal, physical entitiesbut the technology to see them, culture them, and identify them didn't exist yet.

Psychiatry is in an analogous position. The neural, psychological, and social mechanisms underlying mental disorders are realbut we cannot yet directly measure them. We work with descriptions, patterns, and inferences.

What This Means for Your Practice

1
Your clinical skills matter more, not less.

Without definitive tests, your ability to gather, organize, and interpret clinical data is the primary diagnostic instrument.

2
Systematic approaches are essential.

Because we can't "just run a test," we need rigorous frameworks for collecting and weighing evidence.

3
Multiple methods must converge.

Without a single definitive test, we build diagnostic confidence by gathering evidence from different methodspatient interview, validated scales, clinical observations, labsand looking for convergence.

This is why understanding your clinical methodswhat kind of evidence each one provides, where it comes from, and what it can tell usis foundational to psychiatric practice.

The Problem

No single method is sufficient.

So how do you build diagnostic confidence when every method has limitations?

The Weight of Evidence: Direct vs. Indirect Measurement

Not all clinical data carries the same diagnostic weight. The key question is: How directly does this measurement capture the construct we care about?

Direct Measurement

The measurement IS the construct.

TSH = 4.5 mIU/L This IS thyroid function. The number directly tells you the state of the construct.
BP = 160/95 This IS blood pressure. No inference needed.
Troponin = 0.08 This IS cardiac enzyme leveldirect evidence of myocardial damage.

Weight is inherent. A single measurement can carry definitive diagnostic weight because it directly captures the construct.

Indirect Measurement

The measurement INDICATES the construct.

"I feel hopeless" This suggests depression, but doesn't directly measure it. The patient might lack insight, exaggerate, or use words differently.
PHQ-9 = 19 This quantifies reported symptoms, but depression itself isn't being measuredwe're measuring a proxy.
Flat affect observed Consistent with depression, but could also be medication effect, cultural, or baseline personality.

Weight must be established. No single indirect measurement is definitive. We need a way to build confidence.

The Core Challenge

In physical medicine, you often have direct measurements. Troponin > 0.04 carries inherent diagnostic weightyou act on it.

In psychiatry, almost everything is indirect measurement. "Depression" isn't a substance we can measure. We can only observe indicators, descriptions, and proxies.

So how do we establish diagnostic weight for indirect measurements?

In cardiology, an elevated troponin directly measures cardiac damageits weight is inherent. In psychiatry, a patient saying "I feel depressed" is an indirect indicatorits weight must be established.

The question becomes: How do we build diagnostic weight from indirect measurements? When do we have "enough" evidence?

The answer is construct validityand there's a formal framework for establishing it. But first, let's make sure we understand what makes any measurement trustworthy.

Reliability and Validity: The Foundation of Measurement

If subjective quantitative data (like PHQ-9 scores) comes from patient self-report, how can we trust it?

The answer requires understanding two distinct concepts that are often confused:

Reliability
Consistency
"Does it give the same result under the same conditions?"

If a patient's depression hasn't changed, does the PHQ-9 give the same score tomorrow as today?

Validity
Accuracy
"Does it measure what it claims to measure?"

When someone scores 10 on the PHQ-9, do they actually have depression?

Types of Reliability
Test-Retest
Same person, same instrument, different times same result?
PHQ-9 today vs. PHQ-9 in two weeks (if condition unchanged)
Inter-Rater
Different clinicians, same patient same result?
Two clinicians independently rating the same MSE
Internal Consistency
Items within the instrument measuring the same construct?
Do all 9 PHQ items correlate with each other?
The Critical Relationship

Here's what students often miss:

Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity
You CAN have reliability without validity
You CANNOT have validity without reliability

Think about it: if a measure gives you different results every time (unreliable), it can't possibly be accurately measuring anything (invalid). But a measure can be perfectly consistent while consistently measuring the wrong thing.

The Target Analogy
Low Reliability
Low Validity
Scattered everywhere
High Reliability
Low Validity
Tight cluster, wrong spot
High Reliability
High Validity
Tight cluster on target
Notice the Impossible Case

There's no "Low Reliability + High Validity" option. If shots are scattered randomly, they can't all be hitting the bullseye. Consistency is a prerequisite for accuracy.

Reading the Curve
  • Each point represents a different cutoff score
  • Upper-left corner = perfect classification (100% sensitivity, 100% specificity)
  • Diagonal line = random chance (no better than flipping a coin)
  • AUC (Area Under Curve) = overall discriminative ability (0.5 = chance, 1.0 = perfect)
The Cutoff Trade-off

Every cutoff score involves a trade-off between sensitivity and specificity:

Lower Cutoff (PHQ-9 5)
Sensitivity Specificity
Catch nearly everyone with depression... but many false positives
Use when: Missing cases is costly (screening, high-risk populations)
Higher Cutoff (PHQ-9 15)
Sensitivity Specificity
Fewer false alarms... but miss some true cases
Use when: False positives are costly (confirmatory testing, resource-limited settings)

From ROC to Likelihood Ratios

The ROC curve gives us sensitivity and specificity. From these, we can derive something more clinically useful: the Likelihood Ratio.

Positive Likelihood Ratio (LR+)
Sensitivity 1 Specificity
= True Positive Rate / False Positive Rate

What LR+ tells you: How many times more likely is this finding in someone WITH the condition versus someone WITHOUT it?

LR+ = 1
Finding is equally common in cases and non-cases no diagnostic value
LR+ = 2-5
Small increase in probability weak evidence
LR+ = 5-10
Moderate increase in probability useful evidence
LR+ > 10
Large increase in probability strong evidence

From Single Findings to Clinical Patterns

LR+ tells you how much a single finding should shift your thinking. But clinical reasoning isn't about isolated findingsit's about patterns.

When you assess a patient, you gather the same symptom information through multiple methods:

Patient Interview (Patient-Report Qual.)
"I'm exhausted all the time, I have to drag myself out of bed"
PHQ-9 (Patient-Report Quant.)
Item 4 (fatigue) = 3 "nearly every day"
MSE (Clinician-Obs. Qual.)
Psychomotor slowing observed
Labs (Clinician-Obs. Quant.)
TSH = 2.1 (normal) rules out thyroid cause

Notice: you're measuring the same symptom (fatigue) using four different methods.

When they all agree, you can be confident the symptom is realnot artifact, not misunderstanding, not malingering.

This ideathat valid measurement requires convergence across methodswas formalized in 1959.

Campbell & Fiske (1959): The Validity Framework

Psychologists Donald Campbell and Donald Fiske were struggling with a fundamental problem: How do you prove you're actually measuring what you think you're measuring?

Campbell, D.T., & Fiske, D.W. (1959). Convergent and discriminant validation by the multitrait-multimethod matrix. Psychological Bulletin, 56(2), 81-105.

Their answer: measure multiple traits using multiple methods, then look at the pattern.

The MTMM Framework
Multi-Trait
Different things you're trying to detect
Multi-Method
Different ways of gathering information

Discriminant Validity in Practice

You now have the complete framework: base rates give you a starting point, convergent validity confirms symptoms are real, and discriminant validity (LR+) tells you how much each finding should shift your probability.

Now let's apply this systematically using empirical data.

Clinical Examples

High Discriminant Validity
"Feelings of worthlessness and excessive guilt"
Specificity: ~85% LR+: ~6-8

Low Discriminant Validity

The Key Point

Both symptoms are real and clinically important. Fatigue mattersit affects functioning and treatment planning. But when you're building a differential diagnosis, you lean on the high-discriminant findings to narrow your list.

Low-discriminant symptoms confirm something is wrong. High-discriminant symptoms tell you what.

We Did the Analysis

This isn't just theory. We analyzed over 1,400 symptom-disorder pairs across DSM-5-TR disorders using these psychometric methods to determine their discriminant validity.

Here's how we did itand you can follow the logic yourself:

Our Methodology: Using the DSM Against Itself
1
Extract All Diagnostic Criteria
We pulled every symptom from every disorder's diagnostic criteria in the DSM-5-TR. This gave us a complete map of which symptoms belong to which disorders.
2
Count Symptom Overlap
For each symptom, we counted: How many different disorders include this symptom as a criterion?

Example: "Insomnia" appears as a criterion in MDD, GAD, PTSD, Bipolar, Adjustment Disorder, and several others.

Example: "Feelings of worthlessness" appears primarily in MDDit's rare in other disorders.

3
Calculate Specificity from Overlap
The key insight: Specificity is inversely related to how many disorders share a symptom.

If a symptom appears in many disorders LOW specificity it doesn't help you differentiate

If a symptom appears in few disorders HIGH specificity it DOES help you differentiate

4
Calculate Likelihood Ratios
Using the sensitivity and specificity values, we calculated LR+ for each symptom-disorder pair:

LR+ = Sensitivity (1 - Specificity)

5
Apply Base Rate Assumptions
We tested how well symptoms discriminate under different clinical scenarios:
10% Base Rate
General population or primary care setting
How well does this symptom discriminate when the disorder is relatively rare?
30% Base Rate
Psychiatric clinic setting
How well does this symptom discriminate when the disorder is more common?
6
Categorize Discriminant Validity
Based on the LR+ values, we categorized each symptom:
HIGHLY DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ > 30 Strong diagnostic signal; finding makes diagnosis 30+ times more likely
MODERATELY DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ 10-30 Useful signal, but appears in several conditions
LESS DISCRIMINATIVE LR+ < 10 Transdiagnostic; appears across many conditions
Important: These categories help you weight findings. A symptom with LR+ 50 (highly discriminative) shifts your probability 5 more than one with LR+ 10 (moderately discriminative). The "less discriminative" symptoms tell you something is wrong but don't point to a specific diagnosis.
The elegant part: We didn't need external data to do this. The DSM itself tells us which symptoms are specific and which are transdiagnosticwe just had to analyze its own structure systematically. The DSM's criteria reveal their own discriminant validity.

Key Point

Discriminant validity isn't about which symptoms "count" and which don't. All symptoms matter. But symptoms with higher discriminant validity carry more diagnostic weightthey help you differentiate between conditions.

Convergent Validity: Do These Findings Fit Together?

Remember Campbell & Fiske's definition:

Convergent Validity

"The degree to which concepts that should be related theoretically are, in fact, related."

In clinical terms, this translates to:

Do my findings cluster together into a coherent, recognizable pattern?

The Other Half of the Equation

Discriminant validity asks: "Does this finding help me differentiate?"

Convergent validity asks: "Do my findings fit together?"

Discriminant Validity

Read ACROSS the rows

Within each method, do findings distinguish between diagnoses?

Asks: Does this symptom point to THIS diagnosis, not that one? (LR+ tells you)

Convergent Validity

Read DOWN the columns

Across different methods, do findings agree on the diagnosis?

Asks: Do Interview, Scales, MSE, and Labs all point to MDD?

What Does "Convergence" Look Like Clinically?

When findings converge, they point toward the same construct from multiple angles:

Signs of Convergent Validity in Clinical Data
Consistency Across Sources
Patient report matches your observations matches collateral information
Consistency Across Domains
Cognitive symptoms + emotional symptoms + behavioral symptoms + physical symptoms all point the same direction
Temporal Coherence
Symptoms emerged together, wax and wane together, fit a recognizable timeline
Fits the Clinical Picture
The pattern matches what we know about how this disorder presents

What Undermines Convergence?

Low convergent validityfindings that don't fit togetheris a clinical red flag:

Inconsistency across sources: Patient says one thing, you observe another, collateral reports something different
Symptoms from different syndromes: Some findings point to depression, others to mania, others to psychosisthey don't cluster
Temporal incoherence: The timeline doesn't make sense for this disorder
Atypical presentation: The pattern doesn't match what we'd expectconsider alternative diagnoses or comorbidity
Low convergence doesn't mean the patient isn't suffering. It means the clinical picture is complexmaybe there's comorbidity, maybe there's an atypical presentation, maybe you need more information. It's a signal to keep investigating, not to dismiss.

So What Have We Actually Done?

We've arrived at a diagnosis: Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent Episode, Moderate.

Let's be clear about what this is and what it isn't.

The Diagnosis: A Shared Starting Point

The diagnosis is agnostic. It tells us where we arenot where to go. It defines the problem, not the solution.

The DSM isn't perfect. There are many valid criticismscategorical vs. dimensional, cultural bias, comorbidity problems, the medicalization of human suffering. But it is the language we have all agreed on. It's how we communicate with each other, with insurance companies, with the healthcare system.

The diagnosis brings us all to the same starting point. When you say "MDD," another clinician knows what you mean. You can collaborate. You can consult. You can refer. You speak the same language.

Your Role in Getting Here

Think about what it took to arrive at this diagnosis:

Therapeutic rapport. Open-ended questions. Your ability to understand and connect with your patient. Making them feel safe enough to tell you what's really going on. Knowing what to ask, when to probe deeper, when to sit with silence.

All of thatthe art of the clinical interviewis data collection.

It's like the cooking show "Nailed It"everyone's trying to replicate the same design. How you get there, how sound and solid your foundation is, that's between you and your patient. But it is on you to do the best job possible collecting quality data.

The validity framework we've learned gives you a systematic way to evaluate that data. But the quality of your starting point depends on your skill at gathering it.

Where You Go From Here

You can't treat something until you know what you're treating.
