The Fundamental Challenge of Psychiatry

In psychiatry, the "what" we treat‚Äîthe diagnosis‚Äîcannot be measured directly.

We can only describe it.

This is a fundamental difference from much of physical medicine, where the underlying pathology can often be directly observed or tested:

üìú A Historical Parallel

Consider medicine before germ theory was proven and microscopy advanced. Physicians treating infectious disease could:

  • Observe ‚Äî fever, pustules, inflammation, patterns of spread
  • Collect subjective data ‚Äî patient reports of symptoms, onset, severity
  • Recognize patterns ‚Äî certain symptom clusters traveled together
  • Make predictions ‚Äî this pattern usually leads to this outcome

But they could not directly test for the underlying cause. The bacteria were there‚Äîreal, physical entities‚Äîbut the technology to see them, culture them, and identify them didn't exist yet.

Psychiatry is in an analogous position. The neural, psychological, and social mechanisms underlying mental disorders are real‚Äîbut we cannot yet directly measure them. We work with descriptions, patterns, and inferences.

What This Means for Your Practice

1
Your clinical skills matter more, not less.

Without definitive tests, your ability to gather, organize, and interpret clinical data is the primary diagnostic instrument.

2
Systematic approaches are essential.

Because we can't "just run a test," we need rigorous frameworks for collecting and weighing evidence.

3
Validity becomes critical.

We must be deliberate about what our data tells us‚Äîand what it doesn't. Convergent evidence from multiple sources builds the confidence we can't get from a single definitive test.

This is why understanding clinical data types‚Äîwhat kind of evidence we're gathering, where it comes from, and what it can tell us‚Äîis foundational to psychiatric practice.

üí° Why Is There No "Subjective Quantitative"?

Quantitative requires standardized measurement‚Äîwhich requires objectivity. You can't have "your version" of a TSH level. The moment something is measured by an instrument or standardized scale, it becomes objective data. Even a PHQ-9 captures subjective experience but produces objective data‚Äîanyone scoring the same responses gets the same number.

Why Is the Score "Objective"?

The PHQ-9 asks about subjective experiences‚Äîbut the resulting score is considered objective data. How?

üéØ Construct Validity

The instrument has been validated‚Äîresearchers demonstrated that it actually measures the construct it claims to measure (depression severity).

üîÑ Convergent Evidence

PHQ-9 scores correlate with other depression measures (BDI, HAM-D, clinician diagnosis)‚Äîthings that should be related.

‚ÜîÔ∏è Discriminant Evidence

PHQ-9 scores don't correlate with unrelated constructs (extraversion, IQ)‚Äîthings that shouldn't be related.

üìè Standardization

Anyone scoring the same responses gets the same number. The measurement is independent of who administers it.

üîë The Key Insight

Validation transforms subjective content into objective measurement.

The content being measured is subjective (the patient's inner experience). But the measurement process is objective because:

  • The questions are standardized (same for everyone)
  • The scoring is standardized (same rules for everyone)
  • The interpretation is anchored to validated cutoffs
  • The construct validity has been empirically demonstrated

This is why rating scales live in the Objective Quantitative box‚Äînot because the experiences are objective, but because the measurement of those experiences has been validated and standardized.
