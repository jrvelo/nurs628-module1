Treatment Target Individual DBT Skills Group Between-Session Support Why This Component?
Treatment non-adherence — never past 1–2 sessions; left prescriber because “I didn’t like her” The provider–patient dynamic itself helps address this — both in terms of modeling a consistent, validating relationship and in directly working on building skills to manage the discomfort that drives dropout. If it’s a closed group, her non-adherence directly impacts the group dynamic. If open, she could hear others talk about their own push and pull with treatment and learn from their experience. The group may instill hope as she sees peers making progress, and offers universality — which addresses her black-and-white thinking in a different way than individual therapy can. Offers consistent follow-up and a chance to practice boundaries in a lower-stakes context — such as taking the call at an agreed-upon time. Missing a check-in with a peer specialist is lower stakes than missing a therapy session, which gives her room to practice showing up. Individual DBT — addressing therapy-interfering behavior is a core function of individual DBT by design, and the therapeutic relationship is the vehicle. Skills group and between-session support play important supporting roles but can’t replace the direct work on what drives her out of treatment.
