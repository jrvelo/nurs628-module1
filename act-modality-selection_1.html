When Two Modalities Fit — and the Choice Has Stakes

Assignment Overview

You've already done the diagnostic reasoning and built a CBT formulation for D.R. You know this patient. You know his data. You've identified treatment targets under a CBT framework.

This module introduces a different question. Not "does CBT work for D.R.?" — you've already made that case. The question is: does ACT work for D.R. too? And if both fit, how do you choose?

This matters because the meta-analytic evidence says ACT and CBT produce equivalent outcomes for depression. You can't choose between them by looking at effect sizes. You have to choose by reasoning about which modality's mechanism of action best matches the process that's keeping this patient stuck — and where the wrong choice could actually make things worse.

Your completed 5 P's formulation is your starting point. You don't need to redo it. The facts about D.R. haven't changed. What changes is the lens you use to interpret those facts and the intervention that follows.

The Framework — Same Tool, New Application

You've used convergent and discriminant validity twice now:

For diagnosis: Does the evidence converge on MDD? (Convergent.) Does the evidence separate MDD from GAD and Bipolar II? (Discriminant.)
For CBT formulation: Does D.R.'s data converge with what CBT targets? (Convergent.) Does the data separate individual from group as the better format? (Discriminant.)
Now, for modality selection: Does D.R.'s data converge with what ACT targets? (Convergent — Part 1.) Does the data separate CBT from ACT as the better modality for this patient? (Discriminant — Part 2.)

Same reasoning framework each time. Each time applied at a higher level of clinical decision-making.

Part 1: The Convergent Case for ACT

Apply the ACT framework to D.R. and assess whether his data supports psychological inflexibility as a maintaining process.

1A: The ACT Model — Psychological Inflexibility

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) identifies psychological inflexibility as the core pathological process across psychopathology. Where CBT targets the content of cognition (are these thoughts accurate?), ACT targets the person's relationship to their internal experience (what happens when you engage with these thoughts, and does that engagement serve your values?).

The psychological flexibility model identifies six interlocking processes. When these processes become rigid, they constitute psychological inflexibility — the target of ACT intervention.

Experiential Avoidance

Attempts to control, suppress, or escape unwanted internal experiences — even when doing so creates long-term harm.

Cognitive Fusion

Treating thoughts as literal truths rather than mental events. The person doesn't have a thought — the person is the thought.

Loss of Present-Moment Contact

Dominance of the conceptualized past (rumination) or feared future (worry) over flexible attention to the here-and-now.

Attachment to Conceptualized Self

Rigid identification with self-narratives ("I'm a failure," "I'm broken") that become prisons rather than perspectives.

Values Disconnection

Loss of contact with what genuinely matters, replaced by avoidance-driven goals or socially compliant "shoulds."

Behavioral Rigidity

Narrow, avoidant, or going-through-the-motions behavioral patterns disconnected from personal values.

The Key Distinction from CBT

CBT asks: "Is this thought accurate?" and intervenes by restructuring distorted content.

ACT asks: "Is engaging with this thought workable — does it move you toward what matters?" and intervenes by changing the person's relationship to the thought without evaluating its truth value.

These are genuinely different clinical moves. For some patients, the difference determines whether therapy helps or inadvertently reinforces the problem.

▶ ACT's Therapeutic Processes — More Detail

▼

Acceptance — active, open willingness to experience internal events without trying to change their frequency or form. Not resignation — a fundamentally different relationship with experience.

Cognitive Defusion — techniques that alter the function of thoughts by changing the context in which they occur. "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure" vs. "I'm a failure." The thought's content doesn't change; its grip does.

Present Moment Awareness — flexible, voluntary attention to the here-and-now, oriented toward increasing behavioral flexibility.

Self-as-Context — contacting a transcendent sense of self as the observer of experience rather than the content of experience. "You are not your thoughts — you are the awareness in which they occur."

Values — chosen qualities of purposive action. Not goals (achievable, check-off-able) but ongoing directions ("being a present partner" vs. "going on a date Saturday").

Committed Action — building larger patterns of values-consistent behavior, even in the presence of difficult internal experiences.

2B: Modality Selection and Treatment Plan

The Discriminant Question

Based on your analysis, which modality do you recommend as the primary treatment approach for D.R. — CBT or ACT?

Your response should include:

  • What in D.R.'s specific data makes this modality the better process match — not "ACT treats depression" but a formulation-level argument grounded in his clinical picture
  • Process targets and techniques — which specific processes (hexaflex or cognitive triad) are you targeting, in what priority order, and what specific interventions address each target?
  • What in D.R.'s data argues against the alternative — not "CBT doesn't work" but a specific clinical risk or limitation for this patient
  • Evidence: at least one course-assigned resource and one additional peer-reviewed source. The meta-analytic evidence showing equivalence is a starting point, not an endpoint — use the process-specific and comparative evidence to support your reasoning about differential selection
