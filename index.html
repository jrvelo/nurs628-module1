SECTION 1: The Setup

Before we fill in J.M.'s diagnosis, let's step back and ask a basic question: How do we make diagnoses?

What is a diagnosis?

A diagnosis is the identification of a disease or condition. The purpose of diagnosis is to lead us to a plan for how to address itâ€”a treatment plan.

How do we make a diagnosis?

We use diagnostic criteria: a set of signs, symptoms, and tests used to identify a condition.

Diagnostic criteria can take different forms:

  • A single definitive test â€” a lab value, genetic test, or imaging finding that confirms the diagnosis
  • A broader set of criteria â€” when signs and symptoms vary from person to person, we need multiple indicators to accurately identify the condition

SECTION 3: The ROC Curve

The ROC Curve: Seeing the Tradeoff

Sensitivity and specificity are in tension with each other. Lower your threshold â†’ catch more diabetics (higher sensitivity) â†’ but also more false positives (lower specificity). The ROC curve shows this tradeoff:

LR+ = Slope of the line

The steeper the slope from the origin to a point on the curve, the higher the LR+. HbA1c's steep slope = LR+ of 36.

AUC = Overall discrimination

Area Under Curve tells you how well the test separates groups across ALL thresholds. HbA1c's AUC â‰ˆ 0.95 = excellent.

Key insight: A test with high AUC (curve hugging top-left) will have points where you can draw a steep line from the origin â€” meaning high LR+ is achievable. That's why HbA1c works as a single diagnostic test.

Derivation 1: Correlate Rows â†’ Construct Validity

Each disorder has a "profile" â€” a vector of 628 values (has symptom / doesn't have symptom). When we calculate the correlation between two disorders' profiles, we get a measure of how similar they are symptomatically.

This is the modified MTMM approach:

  • High correlation = disorders share many symptoms = potential lack of discriminant validity
  • Low correlation = disorders are symptomatically distinct = good discriminant validity
  • The pattern = which disorders cluster together vs. stand apart

Campbell & Fiske (1959): Two types of validity

Convergent Validity

Things that SHOULD be related ARE related. Disorders in the same family show high correlations.

Discriminant Validity

Things that should NOT be related are NOT related. Different disorder families show low correlations.

What this tells us: Do the DSM diagnostic categories have construct validity? Are they measuring distinct things, or do some of them overlap so much they may be measuring the same underlying thing?
