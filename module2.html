Where We Are

Diagnosis Established

In Module 1, we used the validity framework to arrive at a diagnosis with confidence:

Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent Episode, Moderate (F33.1)

We established construct validity through:

  • Discriminant Validity: High-DV symptoms (worthlessness, guilt) differentiated MDD from other conditions
  • Convergent Validity: Multiple data sources confirmed each criterion; symptoms clustered into a recognizable syndrome

Now What?

We know what J.M. has. How do we decide what to do about it?

The diagnosis tells us where we areit's our shared starting point. But MDD can be treated many different ways:

  • Medications (SSRIs, SNRIs, atypicals...)
  • Individual therapy (CBT, DBT, IPT, psychodynamic, ACT, IFS...)
  • Group therapy
  • Family therapy
  • Lifestyle interventions (exercise, sleep hygiene, nutrition...)
  • Neuromodulation (TMS, ECT...)
  • Combined approaches

How do we choose? This is where the 5 Ps framework becomes essential.

The 5 Ps Framework

Before we apply this to treatment, let's make sure we're clear on what each "P" represents:

Notice the temporal dimension: The 5 Ps move from DISTAL (distant past) to PROXIMAL (recent/current). This matters for treatmentdifferent therapies target different time frames.

P Presenting Problem PROXIMAL / NOW
"What brought them here?"
The current symptoms, their severity, and functional impact. What the patient is experiencing right now.
P Predisposing Factors DISTAL / PAST
"What made them vulnerable?"
Historical factors that increased risk:
  • Biological: Genetics, family psychiatric history, medical conditions
  • Developmental: Developmental delays, learning differences, early trauma
  • Psychological: Attachment patterns, personality traits, cognitive styles
  • Social/ACEs: Adverse childhood experiences, unstable home, neglect, abuse
  • Prior episodes: History of this or related conditions
P Precipitating Factors PROXIMAL / RECENT
"What triggered this? Why now?"
Recent events or stressors that triggered this episode. What changed in the weeks/months before onsetthe "last straw" that activated vulnerabilities.
P Perpetuating Factors PROXIMAL / ONGOING
"What's keeping it going?"
Current factors maintaining the problem: cognitive patterns, avoidance behaviors, interpersonal dynamics, environmental stressors, physiological cycles. These are often your primary therapy targets.
P Protective Factors DISTAL + PROXIMAL
"What strengths can we build on?"
Resources for recoveryboth historical foundations and current assets:
  • Developmental/Historical: Stable childhood home, secure attachment, no significant ACEs
  • Educational: Highest level of education, intellectual resources, problem-solving capacity
  • Social: Current support network, quality of relationships, community ties
  • Psychological: Insight, motivation, coping skills, resilience, prior treatment response
  • Functional: Stable employment, housing, financial resources
Key insight: You already organized J.M.'s data for diagnosis. Now you'll organize the same data for treatment planning. Same information, different lens, different purpose.

Now that you've organized J.M.'s data into the 5 Ps, let's see what each category tells us about treatment:

How Each "P" Guides Treatment
PRESENTING What needs immediate attention?
  • PHQ-9: 19 (moderately severe)
  • No suicidal ideation outpatient appropriate
  • Functional impairment: social withdrawal, difficulty at work
  • 15-lb weight loss over 4 months
Treatment implication: Moderate severity suggests outpatient therapy appropriate. Weight loss warrants monitoring. May benefit from combined treatment (therapy + medication) given severity.
PREDISPOSING What vulnerabilities inform our approach?
  • Prior depressive episode (15 years ago)
  • Family history: depression (mother), completed suicide (maternal uncle)
  • Tendency toward self-critical thinking patterns
Treatment implication: Recurrent episode = higher relapse risk; consider maintenance treatment. Strong family history supports biological component medication may be indicated. Self-critical patterns suggest cognitive therapy approaches.
PRECIPITATING What triggered this? What does that tell us?
  • Youngest son moved out 4 months ago
  • "Empty nest" life transition
  • Loss of primary role as active parent
Treatment implication: Role transition is a core trigger suggests Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) may be particularly relevant. Also need to explore identity/meaning beyond parenting role.
PERPETUATING What's keeping it going? (Primary therapy target)
  • Self-critical cognitions: "I failed as a mother"
  • Social withdrawal avoiding friends and activities
  • Ruminative thinking patterns
  • Sleep/appetite disruption creating fatigue cycle
Treatment implication: These are your therapy targets. Self-critical cognitions CBT (cognitive restructuring). Social withdrawal Behavioral Activation. Rumination mindfulness-based approaches. Sleep/appetite behavioral interventions + possibly medication.
PROTECTIVE What can we build on? (Distal + Proximal)

Historical/Developmental (Distal):

  • Supportive parents and sister
  • Stable 20+ year career as accountant
  • No substance use
  • Prior positive response to sertraline + therapy
  • Seeking help insight present
Treatment implication: Stable developmental history and absence of ACEs = better prognosis, less likely to need trauma-focused work. Strong current social support is a resource. Education level supports psychoeducation and cognitive approaches. Prior treatment response guides approach. Insight supports therapy engagement.

The Point

Social history isn't just one category.

The same domain of informationfamily, relationships, substances, workcan sort into any of the 5 Ps depending on:

  • When Is it distal (past) or proximal (recent/current)?
  • Function Does it create vulnerability? Trigger episodes? Maintain problems? Provide resources?

This is why you can't just "collect social history" and be done. You have to think about what it means and where it fits.

The Logic Behind the Categories: Bayes Returns

Why does "stable childhood" go in Protective while "chaotic childhood" goes in Predisposing? Why does "16 years sober" protect while "urges to drink" perpetuates?

It's not arbitrary. It's Bayesian logicthe same reasoning we used for diagnosis.

Remember from Module 1:

Every piece of clinical data either increases or decreases the probability of something. That's what made symptoms useful for diagnosisthey changed our probability estimates.

The same logic applies herebut to a different question.

The 5 Ps Are Organized by Probability Direction

PREDISPOSING
Increases Probability

Data that increases the probability of developing the condition.

"Chaotic childhood" higher probability of attachment difficulties, emotion dysregulation, vulnerability to depression

PROTECTIVE
Decreases Probability

Data that decreases the probability of poor outcomes.

"Stable childhood" lower probability of attachment difficulties, better foundation, more resilience

PRECIPITATING
Activated the Vulnerability

The event that converted vulnerability into active illness.

"Current divorce" given her childhood history, this specific stressor activated the latent vulnerability

PERPETUATING
Maintains Elevated Probability

Factors that keep the probability of symptoms elevated.

"Ongoing conflict with ex" continues to feed the depression, prevents recovery

This is why the same data can go in different Ps. "Divorce" as a childhood event increased K.R.'s baseline vulnerability (predisposing). "Divorce" happening now activated that vulnerability (precipitating). "Conflict with ex" keeps the symptoms going (perpetuating). It's all about when it happened and what probability it changes.

Connecting the Dots

Module 1: We used Bayesian reasoning to ask "What does she have?" symptoms changed our probability estimates about diagnosis.

Module 2: We use the same logic to ask "Why does she have it?" history, context, and circumstances change our probability estimates about vulnerability, triggers, and prognosis.

The framework is the same. The question is different.

Same Data, Different Lens

You collected all this data in your assessment. But how you organize itand what you pay attention todepends on the question you're asking.

For DIAGNOSIS, we organized by:
  • Quantitative vs. Qualitative PHQ-9 score of 19 vs. "feels empty"
  • Subjective vs. Objective Patient report vs. MSE observations
  • Positive AND Negative findings What's present AND what's absent
What mattered: Symptoms present, symptoms absent (no mania, no psychosis), convergence across sources, discriminant validity

Negative findings were just as important "Denies manic episodes" helped rule OUT bipolar
For FORMULATION, we organize by:
  • The 5 Ps Presenting, Predisposing, Precipitating, Perpetuating, Protective
  • Temporal: Distal vs. Proximal Distant past vs. recent/current
  • Function What role does this data play?
What matters: Context, vulnerabilities, what triggered this, what's maintaining it, what resources exist

History becomes central Childhood stability (or instability) shapes the whole picture

Example "No history of childhood abuse or neglect":

For diagnosis? Barely relevant. Not in MDD criteria. Noted and moved on.

For formulation? Highly relevant. Tells us about developmental foundationit's a protective factor.

Example "Denies manic/hypomanic episodes":

For diagnosis? Crucial. A negative finding that ruled OUT bipolar.

For formulation? Less central. Diagnosis is established. Doesn't tell us about vulnerabilities or context.

The data is all there in your assessment.
The clinical question determines which lens you useand which data moves to the foreground.

Notice: The 5 Ps don't give you one "right answer." They give you a rationale for your treatment choices. You can defend why you chose CBT (perpetuating cognitions), why you might add medication (severity + history), why IPT could be valuable (precipitating role transition). The framework makes your clinical reasoning explicit and defensible.

A Possible Treatment Plan for J.M.

Initial Phase:

  • Restart sertraline (prior positive response) coordinate with prescriber
  • Weekly individual therapy using CBT framework
  • Early focus: Behavioral activation to address withdrawal

Middle Phase:

  • Cognitive restructuring targeting self-critical thoughts
  • Explore role transition and identity beyond motherhood (IPT elements)
  • Introduce mindfulness techniques for rumination

Later Phase:

  • Relapse prevention planning (given recurrent history)
  • Consider involving family for support
  • Transition to maintenance

This treatment plan isn't the only right answerbut it's a defensible one, grounded in J.M.'s specific 5 Ps rather than generic "depression treatment."
